Undertone Guide

The complete guide to makeup for olive skin

The most misunderstood undertone in beauty, finally explained. Why your foundation looks orange, what colors actually flatter you, and how to shade-match once and for all.

18 min read • Updated Feb 2026

Quick Answer

Olive undertones are defined by a greenish or grayish-yellow cast in the skin that falls outside the traditional warm/cool/neutral spectrum. This is why standard foundation shade-matching fails for olive skin — warm shades pull orange and cool shades pull pink. The key to getting makeup right on olive skin is choosing muted, earthy tones (dusty rose, terracotta, taupe, olive green, muted mauve) and seeking out foundations with yellow-green bases rather than orange-warm or pink-cool ones. Olive skin exists across all ethnicities and depths, from very fair to very deep.

Why olive skin is the hardest to shade-match

If you have ever stood in a store swiping foundation after foundation on your jaw only to see each one turn orange, pink, or oddly gray within minutes, you probably have olive undertones. And you are not imagining it — the beauty industry has historically built its shade systems around three categories: warm (golden/peachy), cool (pink/red), and neutral (a mix of both). Olive doesn't fit neatly into any of these.

Olive undertones are caused by a higher presence of natural green pigment in the skin, likely from a combination of melanin distribution, hemoglobin levels, and carotenoid concentration. This greenish or grayish-yellow cast is subtle enough that many people live their entire lives without realizing they are olive — they just know that makeup never quite looks right.

The core problem is this: when foundations are formulated for "warm" skin, they are loaded with orange and peach pigments designed to complement golden undertones. But olive skin doesn't have a golden base — it has a green one. Those orange pigments sit on top of your green-leaning complexion and create an obvious clash. On the other hand, "cool" foundations contain pink pigments that similarly fight against your natural cast, leaving you looking flushed or ruddy in all the wrong places.

The good news? The beauty industry has finally started acknowledging olive as a legitimate undertone category. More brands are releasing olive-specific shades, and once you understand the rules of olive skin, you can navigate the existing shade landscape with confidence.

Olive undertones are defined by a greenish or grayish-yellow cast visible in natural light.

How to identify olive undertones

Olive skin is not a skin depth — it is an undertone. You can be very fair with olive undertones (common in people of Mediterranean, Eastern European, or East Asian descent) or very deep with olive undertones (common in South Asian, Middle Eastern, Latinx, and some African complexions). Here are the most reliable ways to determine if you are olive:

1

The vein test (modified)

Look at the veins on your inner wrist in natural daylight. Most guides say blue veins equal cool and green veins equal warm. But if your veins appear teal, muted green, or a murky mix that you genuinely cannot categorize as blue or green, that ambiguity itself is a strong indicator of olive undertones. Olive skin makes veins appear more muted and harder to read than purely warm or cool skin.

2

The white fabric test

Hold a piece of pure white fabric or paper against your bare, clean face in natural light. If your skin appears to have a faint greenish, grayish, or ashy cast next to the white (rather than looking clearly golden-warm or pink-cool), you likely have olive undertones. This test is especially revealing on the neck and chest area.

3

The foundation fail test

This is the most practical test of all. If "warm" foundations consistently look orange or too peachy on you, and "cool" foundations look too pink or frosty, and even "neutral" shades don't seem quite right — you are almost certainly olive. No other undertone category produces this universal shade mismatch.

4

The jewelry test (refined)

Classic advice says warm skin suits gold and cool skin suits silver. Olive skin often looks best in antique gold, rose gold, brass, bronze, or champagne metals — metals with a muted, slightly tarnished quality. Bright, shiny yellow gold can look garish, and bright silver can look too stark. If you gravitate toward muted, vintage-looking metals, that is an olive indicator.

5

The tan test

Olive skin tends to tan easily and turns a deeper golden-green or warm brown rather than turning pink or red first. When your tan fades, you may notice a slightly grayish or sallow quality to your skin. If people describe your complexion as "sallow" or if your skin looks slightly green-tinged when you are tired or unwell, these are hallmarks of olive undertones.

The olive skin color palette

Once you understand that olive skin's defining trait is its muted green-yellow cast, choosing the right makeup colors becomes intuitive. The guiding principle is simple: muted, earthy, slightly "dirty" tones complement olive skin, while bright, neon, or extremely cool-toned shades fight against it.

Colors that flatter olive skin

  • Dusty rose — The perfect blush and lip color for olive skin. Not bright pink, not coral — a soft, muted rose with a slightly brownish undertone that harmonizes beautifully with green-leaning skin.
  • Terracotta and burnt sienna — Warm earth tones that add warmth without pulling orange. Stunning as eyeshadow, bronzer, and lip color. Terracotta is olive skin's most universally flattering shade.
  • Taupe and mushroom — The ideal neutral for olive tones. Taupe eyeshadow replaces the browns that can look too warm and the grays that look too cold. It is the perfect everyday lid color.
  • Olive green and khaki — Yes, olive on olive works. Green-based eyeshadows, eyeliners, and even mascaras create a tonal harmony that is effortlessly chic.
  • Muted mauve and plum — Purple-leaning shades that have been desaturated or "dirtied" work exceptionally well. Think wine, not grape. Plum, not violet.
  • Warm bronze and copper — Metallic shades in the bronze-copper family add a gorgeous luminosity to olive skin, especially on the eyelids. These warm metallics enhance the natural depth of olive complexions.
  • Caramel and toffee — For lip colors, brownish-warm nudes in the caramel family are far more flattering than standard beige nudes. These add warmth without the orange problem.

Colors to approach with caution

  • Bright coral — The orange base in coral fights directly with olive's green undertone. If you love coral, choose a very muted, brownish version.
  • Hot pink and fuchsia — Extremely cool-toned pinks can make olive skin look sallow or grayish by contrast. Opt for muted raspberry instead.
  • Pastel anything — Pale pastels tend to wash olive skin out, making it look ashy or flat. If you want lighter shades, choose ones with a warm mute — think "dusty" pastels rather than clean ones.
  • True orange — Orange lipstick and orange-based blush is one of the trickiest colors for olive skin. The clash with green undertones is immediate and obvious.
  • Cool silver eyeshadow — Bright silver can look metallic and harsh against olive skin. Swap for pewter or gunmetal, which have a warmer edge.

Foundation matching tips for olive skin

Foundation is where olive skin faces its biggest challenge. Here is a detailed approach to finding your match:

1

Look for olive-specific shade lines

Several brands have acknowledged olive undertones in their shade development. These are the most reliable starting points:

Kosas Revealer Foundation — olive-labeled shades NARS Sheer Glow — shades like Deauville, Barcelona, Syracuse Fenty Beauty — shades with the "olive" descriptor in shade finder MAC Studio Fix — NC shades mixed with a drop of green corrector
2

Use the yellow-base rule

When you cannot find an explicitly olive shade, choose foundations with a strong yellow base rather than an orange-warm or pink-cool base. Yellow sits closest to olive on the color wheel. A yellow-based "neutral-warm" shade will often work better than anything labeled simply "warm."

3

The green mixer trick

This is the olive skin secret weapon. Buy a green or blue color-correcting mixer (LA Girl makes an affordable one, NYX has one too) and add one to two drops into any too-warm foundation. This neutralizes the orange cast and pushes the shade toward olive. It is a game-changer if you have already found a shade that matches your depth but not your undertone.

Budget: LA Girl Pro Color Foundation Mixing Pigment in Blue (~$7) Mid: NYX Total Control Drop Foundation Mixer Splurge: Danessa Myricks Vision Cream Cover in Olive mixer
4

Test on your neck, not your jaw

For olive skin, the jawline test is unreliable because the jaw area often has different undertones from the rest of your face. Instead, swatch on the side of your neck below your ear. This area tends to be less affected by sun exposure and shows your true undertone. Wait five full minutes before judging — many foundations oxidize and shift warmer on olive skin.

5

Consider tinted moisturizer instead

If shade-matching full-coverage foundation feels impossible, consider switching to a sheer tinted moisturizer or skin tint. These formulas are forgiving enough that a slightly imperfect shade match becomes invisible on skin. Your natural undertone shows through, which is exactly what you want.

Budget: Colourpop Pretty Fresh Tinted Moisturizer (~$14) Mid: Ilia Super Serum Skin Tint (~$48) Splurge: Saie Slip Tint (~$35)

The olive skin everyday routine

This 7-minute routine is designed specifically around the unique needs and flattering tones of olive skin. Every product choice accounts for the green undertone factor.

1

Color-corrected base

Start with your tinted moisturizer or foundation. If your shade runs slightly warm, mix in a tiny drop of blue or green mixer. Apply with fingers for the most natural finish — the warmth of your hands helps the product meld into olive skin seamlessly.

2

Concealer in a yellow-toned shade

For undereye circles (which often appear greenish-brown on olive skin), choose a concealer with a peach-yellow correcting tone — not straight orange, which will be too harsh. Dab and blend with your ring finger.

3

Bronzer in matte terracotta

Skip orange-toned bronzers entirely. A matte terracotta or warm-brown bronzer with zero orange gives olive skin a sun-kissed warmth that looks natural. Apply in the hollows of cheeks and along the temples.

Budget: Physician's Formula Butter Bronzer (~$12) Mid: Rare Beauty Warm Wishes Bronzer Stick (~$26)
4

Dusty rose blush

A muted, slightly brownish rose on the apples and cheekbones. This shade family is the single most universally flattering blush category for olive skin across all depths. Cream formulas blend especially well.

Budget: Milani Baked Blush in Berry Amore (~$9) Mid: Rare Beauty Soft Pinch in Joy or Bliss (~$23)
5

Taupe eyeshadow + brown mascara

A single taupe or mushroom shade across the lid gives olive eyes quiet definition without the heaviness of black or the warmth-clash of orange-brown. Brown mascara looks softer and more natural on olive skin than jet black.

6

Mauve-nude lip

A muted mauve-brown or dusty rose lip balm or lipstick ties everything together. This is your "my lips but better" shade family — close to your natural lip color with a slightly warmer, rosier push.

Budget: Maybelline Color Sensational in Touchable Taupe (~$8) Mid: MAC Matte Lipstick in Taupe or Whirl (~$23)

Olive skin across different depths

Because olive is an undertone rather than a depth, the specific shade recommendations vary depending on how light or deep your complexion is:

  • Fair olive — Often found in Mediterranean, Eastern European, and some East Asian backgrounds. Skin may look sallow or slightly grayish-green in winter. Look for foundations in the lightest "warm-neutral" ranges and lean toward softer dusty rose, light taupe, and champagne shades.
  • Light-medium olive — The most common depth where olive undertones are identified. Foundation in the medium "neutral-warm" range works best. This is where terracotta, mushroom, and muted copper shades shine brightest.
  • Medium-tan olive — Common in Middle Eastern, South Asian, and Latinx complexions. The green undertone may be less immediately obvious but becomes apparent when foundations pull orange. Rich warm plums, deep dusty roses, and warm browns are your best friends.
  • Deep olive — The green undertone in deep skin can read as a cool-neutral quality that makes standard warm-deep foundations look too red or orange. Look for foundations in deep ranges with muted, neutral-warm undertones. Berry tones, deep terracotta, and rich mauves complement deep olive beautifully.

Common mistakes on olive skin

  • Using orange-based bronzer — The number-one mistake. Orange bronzer on olive skin creates a muddy, unnatural stripe. Always choose matte bronzers with a brown or terracotta base.
  • Matching to the wrong season — Olive skin changes noticeably between summer (deeper, more golden) and winter (lighter, more green-gray). You likely need two foundation shades or a mixer to adjust year-round.
  • Skipping color correction — Olive skin dark circles have a unique greenish-brown cast that standard peach correctors cannot fully address. Layer a yellow-toned corrector beneath your concealer for best results.
  • Choosing "warm" defaults — Being told you are "warm" and selecting all warm-labeled products is the fastest path to looking orange. You are olive, and that means muted-warm at best, not standard warm.
  • Ignoring muted tones — Bright, saturated colors can overwhelm olive undertones. Seek out "muted," "dusty," and "smoky" versions of your favorite shades for the most harmonious results.

Frequently asked questions

Am I warm-toned or olive-toned?

Warm and olive are often confused, but they are distinct. Warm undertones are purely golden or peachy. Olive undertones have a greenish or grayish-yellow cast that is visible in natural light. The easiest test: hold a pure yellow fabric against your face. If your skin looks slightly green or grayish beside it rather than harmonizing, you are likely olive. Another clue: if foundations labeled "warm" pull orange on you and foundations labeled "cool" look too pink, you almost certainly have olive undertones.

Why does foundation look orange on me?

Foundation looks orange on olive skin because most "warm" foundations are formulated with orange and peach pigments to counteract blue undertones. But olive skin does not have blue undertones — it has green ones. The orange pigments in warm foundations clash with your natural green cast, creating an unnatural orange mask. The fix: look for foundations specifically formulated for olive undertones, or choose shades described as "neutral-warm" with yellow (not orange) bases. Brands like Kosas, NARS, and Fenty have olive-friendly ranges.

What is the best blush color for olive skin?

The best blush colors for olive skin are muted, earthy tones rather than bright or neon shades. Dusty rose, muted mauve, soft terracotta, warm plum, and tawny peach all look beautiful. Avoid bright coral, hot pink, or very cool-toned pinks — these can look unnatural against olive undertones. Berry shades work well for deeper olive tones. The key principle: choose blush colors that look slightly "dirty" or muted in the pan. What looks dull in the packaging often comes alive on olive skin.

Can olive skin wear nude lipstick?

Absolutely, but the right nude for olive skin is different from what is marketed as "universal nude." Most nude lipsticks lean too pink or too beige, which can wash olive skin out or look ashy. Look for nudes with a brownish-mauve, dusty rose, or warm caramel base. Muted mauvy-pinks and soft terracotta nudes are particularly flattering. Avoid anything too pale, too cool-pink, or too white-based. A nude that is one or two shades deeper than your natural lip color with a warm-muted undertone will look most natural.
